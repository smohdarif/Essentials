## Tarassul in Adhan and Hadar in Iqamah

The teacher began by continuing the discussion on adhan from the previous class. The topic was the reading style of the adhan vs the iqamah.

### What is Tarassul?

**Tarassul** comes from the root meaning "to loosen" or "to leave hanging" -- like hair that hangs down from the head (mutarasil). In the context of adhan, it means to **read it slowly, with pauses** between the kalimat.

So the muadhin says **Allahu Akbar**, pauses, then **Allahu Akbar**, pauses -- reading each phrase deliberately and with gaps in between.

### What is Hadar?

**Hadar** means to make it **a bit faster** -- not rushing, but you are not stopping between the same kalimat. The teacher compared it to the styles of Quran recitation (tilawah), where there is **tartil** (slow, measured), **hadar** (faster), and a third even quicker style.

### The Ruling

- **Adhan:** Read with tarassul -- slow, deliberate, with pauses
- **Iqamah:** Read with hadar -- a bit faster, without long pauses

If someone doesn't follow this -- say, he reads the iqamah slowly like the adhan -- it is still **valid (ja'iz)**, but it goes against the **istihbab** (recommended way). The teacher noted that going against istihbab doesn't bring karahah; at most, it is a very slight dislike.

### The Hanafi Position on Muthanna (Paired Reading)

The hadith mentions **muthanna** -- reading the phrases of adhan "once once" (i.e., each phrase individually). There's a discussion about what this means. The Hanafi interpretation is that **muthanna** means you join the two phrases into one sentence:

> **اللهُ أَكْبَرُ اللهُ أَكْبَرُ** -- this counts as one

So you are not saying it separately as two, but rather joining them together to read as one combined phrase. However, the teacher acknowledged that this answer is not the strongest, because another narration clearly states each phrase is read "once once" except for the **takbir at the beginning**, which is read twice. The better answer is that the hadith which is **mashhur** (well-known) and has more narrators supports the standard Hanafi adhan, and that takes precedence.

## Facing the Qibla During Adhan

It is **sunnah** for the muadhin to face the qibla when giving adhan. Why? Because the angel who came down from the skies and taught the adhan -- he gave the adhan **whilst facing the qibla**.

However, if the muadhin leaves facing the qibla, the adhan is still **valid (ja'iz)**. It is not wajib. The main objective of adhan is **ilam** -- to notify and inform people that the time of salah has come and to call them for jama'ah. That objective is achieved regardless of which direction the muadhin faces.

## Turning the Face for Hayya ala al-Salah and Hayya ala al-Falah

When the muadhin reaches **حَيَّ عَلَى الصَّلَاةِ** and **حَيَّ عَلَى الْفَلَاحِ**, he turns:

- His **face to the right** for one
- His **face to the left** for the other

**Important:** He turns **only his face** -- not his chest. His body remains facing the qibla throughout. Only the face turns.

### How Much Does He Turn?

There are two opinions:

1. Some scholars said **حَيَّ عَلَى الصَّلَاةِ** is said starting from the right all the way, sweeping to the left, and **حَيَّ عَلَى الْفَلَاحِ** continues from the left back to the right
2. Others said he simply turns to the **right** for Hayya ala al-Salah and to the **left** for Hayya ala al-Falah

The **Sahib al-Hidaya** has given **tarjeeh** (preference) to the second opinion -- a person should say both phrases (Hayya ala al-Salah and Hayya ala al-Falah) on **both sides**, right and left.

### Why Only Turn for These Phrases? -- A Beautiful Point

The teacher shared a point he really liked. If you look at the adhan, it has **three parts**:

1. **First part** -- **اللهُ أَكْبَرُ** (Allahu Akbar) and the takbirat → This is **munajat** (intimate conversation with Allah), speaking of Allah's greatness
2. **Middle part** -- **حَيَّ عَلَى الصَّلَاةِ** and **حَيَّ عَلَى الْفَلَاحِ** → This is **munadat** (calling to the people), actually inviting them to come
3. **Last part** -- **لَا إِلٰهَ إِلَّا اللهُ** → This is again **munajat**, turning back to Allah

The turning towards the people only happens in the **second portion** (munadat) because that's the part where you're actually **calling** the people. In the first and last portions, you are in munajat with Allah -- just like in salah, you face the qibla. So you remain facing the qibla for those parts.

## The Minaret (Manara) and Giving Adhan from a High Place

### What is a Soma'ah?

The teacher explained the origin of the word. A **soma'ah** (صَوْمَعَة) originally referred to the small, elevated cells that **monks (ruhban)** would build. They would construct these high, small rooms so that when they went up for their worship, they wouldn't be disturbed.

This was then adopted for the **manara** (minaret) -- the tall structure from which the muadhin calls the adhan.

### Why High?

The higher the position, the **farther the sound travels**. Sound travels in waves, and when those waves collide with objects, the sound reduces and doesn't carry as far. By elevating the source, there is less collision, and the sound reaches a wider area. That's also why today, **speakers are placed at the top** of minarets.

### From the Seerah

The teacher mentioned several narrations:

- **Bilal** (may Allah be pleased with him) would **climb up onto a roof** to give the adhan
- One **Sahabiyyah** (a lady from Bani Najar) said: *"My house was the tallest"* -- and Bilal would come to her roof. In the time of suhoor, he would sit on the roof, watch for subh sadiq (true dawn), and then read certain kalimat he never missed:

> **اللَّهُمَّ بِحَمْدِكَ...**

- Another hadith mentions that Bilal had a **pillar ('amud)** or would use **steps or sticks** to climb up to a higher position

So it is **sunnah** to give adhan from an elevated place. Today, the speakers on the minaret fulfill this purpose -- the muadhin doesn't need to physically climb up anymore.

### A Side Note: Where Did Adhan Originate?

The teacher asked the students: did the adhan come in Makkah or Madinah? The **fard of salah** was legislated in **Makkah** during the Mi'raj -- and the Mi'raj happened after the Prophet's (peace be upon him) visit to Ta'if, when the people of Ta'if rejected him and sent street boys to pelt him with stones. After that painful experience, Allah elevated him and gave him the consolation of Mi'raj.

But the **adhan itself** was only established in **Madinah** -- after the hijrah, when the Muslim community needed a way to call people to congregational prayer.

## Going Around the Minaret (Istidara)

The sunnah is to **stand in one place** and turn your face to the right and left. But what if the minaret is large and the muadhin can't reach all corners just by turning his face?

In that case, he is allowed to **walk around** the minaret -- but only during **حَيَّ عَلَى الصَّلَاةِ** and **حَيَّ عَلَى الْفَلَاحِ**. He tries to cover all 360 degrees so the voice reaches all directions (**jawanib**).

However, if there is **no need** (no hajjah) for going around -- meaning the sound already reaches everywhere -- then it is **not good** to walk around. Because the sunnah is to stand in one place and turn only the face. Walking around without need would go against the sunnah.

## Placing Fingers in the Ears During Adhan

It is **better (ahsan)** for the muadhin to place both **index fingers in his ears** while giving adhan. This was what the Prophet (peace be upon him) commanded.

### Why?

Because it helps the voice **reach farther** (ablagh). When you put your fingers in your ears, you feel like your voice is low, so you naturally **raise your voice** to compensate. This makes the adhan louder and more far-reaching.

### But If He Doesn't?

If the muadhin doesn't put his fingers in his ears, it's **not a problem** (fahasan -- "then it's fine"). Why?

The teacher gave a precise distinction:

- The **sunan of adhan** are those things that relate to the **kalimat** (words) of adhan -- the actual phrases and their order
- Putting fingers in the ears is not about the kalimat -- it's about the **sawt** (voice)
- Since it relates to the voice and not the actual structure of the adhan, it is **not from the actual sunan of adhan**

The teacher also mentioned a second reason: it was **not mentioned** in the hadith of Abdullah ibn Abd Rabbih (the dream where the angel taught the adhan). Since it wasn't part of what the angel instructed, it further supports that it's not from the core sunan of adhan -- it's a helpful practice related to voice projection.

## The Mas'alah of Tathweeb

This was the major topic of the lesson. The teacher noted there has been a lot of discussion about this among both **Mutaqaddimeen** (earlier scholars) and **Muta'akhkhireen** (later scholars).

### What is Tathweeb?

**Tathweeb** literally means **to return** (ruju'a) -- to go back and repeat something. It also carries the meaning that the **benefit returns** to the person who does it.

In fiqhi terms, tathweeb means: **repeating a call to prayer** -- specifically, saying:

> **الصَّلَاةُ خَيْرٌ مِنَ النَّوْمِ**

or similar phrases to remind and call people to salah **between the adhan and iqamah**.

### Tathweeb in Fajr

In the time of **Fajr**, the muadhin or anyone else can say **الصَّلَاةُ خَيْرٌ مِنَ النَّوْمِ** (twice) between the adhan and iqamah. This is considered **good and recommended**.

Why specifically Fajr? Because Fajr is the time of **sleep and heedlessness (ghaflah)**. People are most likely to miss this prayer.

### The Sunnah of the Prophet (peace be upon him) After Fajr Adhan

The teacher shared a beautiful detail from the Seerah: after the Fajr adhan, the Prophet (peace be upon him) would pray the **two rakats of Fajr sunnah**, then he would **lie down**. His position was: the **elbow on the ground**, with the **head resting on the palm** of the hand. He would sleep for a very short time because he had spent so much of the night in **Tahajjud**.

The teacher also mentioned reading in a book on Seerah that after Fajr, the Prophet (peace be upon him) would visit the houses of his wives, and then go to whichever wife's turn it was (**nawbah**) and rest there, because he had spent so much time in Tahajjud during the night.

Then Bilal (may Allah be pleased with him) would come and say the tathweeb -- calling him for salah.

### Tathweeb in Other Prayers -- Makruh or Ja'iz?

For prayers **other than Fajr**, the earlier ruling was that tathweeb is **makruh**. Why? Because in the Seerah and the practice of the Sahaba, tathweeb was only done for Fajr -- the time of sleep.

**However**, the later scholars (**Muta'akhkhireen**) said: in today's times, the **ghaflah** (heedlessness) and materialism have increased so much that people are absorbed in the dunya and forget about salah times -- not just at Fajr, but at **all times**. Therefore, the fatwa now is that **tathweeb is ja'iz (permissible) in all the salawat**.

The teacher compared this to what the **Tablighi Jama'at** does in the markaz -- after salah or before salah, they make a round (**chakkar**), going around to remind people to come for jama'ah. This is essentially the same concept as tathweeb.

### What Words Should Be Used?

There is no restriction to specific words. Whatever is **customary** ('urf) among the people can be used. It could be:

- **الصَّلَاةُ الصَّلَاةُ** ("Prayer! Prayer!")
- **حَيَّ عَلَى الصَّلَاةِ حَيَّ عَلَى الْفَلَاحِ**
- Or even just: "Brother, let's go for salah"

Whatever the community is accustomed to -- that is acceptable. And this is what the fatwa is upon.

### Tathweeb for the Amir, Qadi, and Mufti

**Imam Muhammad** (rahimahullah) had an interesting position. He said it is **munasib** (appropriate) to specifically go and inform the **Amir al-Mu'mineen** (leader), the **Qadi** (judge), and the **Mufti** about the salah time. Why? Because these people are extremely busy with the affairs of the Muslim community -- solving people's problems and disputes. They are most likely to lose track of time and miss the **takbirah ula** (first takbir of jama'ah).

The teacher said he's personally seen this with great scholars -- their **khadim** (attendant) or close associate would come and tell them: *"Hazrat, three minutes remaining for salah."* This way, if the scholar has visitors, he can gracefully end the meeting: *"Let's go for salah."*

### Imam Abu Hanifah's Response

**Imam Abu Hanifah** (rahimahullah) took a slightly different view. He said: why are you specifying only the Amir? **All people are the same** when it comes to jama'ah. If you're going to remind someone, remind everyone.

The teacher noted that Imam Abu Hanifah's view is a bit strict (**dry**, as he put it), because in practice, Bilal (may Allah be pleased with him) would specifically go to the Prophet (peace be upon him) -- he wouldn't go to every single person. So the practice of specifying certain people has a **dalil** (proof).

The reconciliation is: the Sahib al-Hidaya specified the Amir, Qadi, and Mufti because they are the most occupied with community affairs and thus most at risk of missing salah. This doesn't mean others are excluded -- the tathweeb is for everyone, but there's special emphasis on those who are busy serving the ummah.

## Quick Summary

- **Tarassul (slow, paused reading) is sunnah for adhan**; **Hadar (faster reading) is sunnah for iqamah** -- doing otherwise is ja'iz but against istihbab
- **Muthanna** in the Hanafi interpretation means joining the paired phrases into one, though the stronger proof is the mashhur hadith with more narrators
- **Facing the qibla during adhan is sunnah**, based on the angel facing the qibla -- but leaving it doesn't invalidate the adhan
- **Only the face turns** for Hayya ala al-Salah and Hayya ala al-Falah -- the chest stays towards the qibla
- The adhan has **three parts**: munajat (takbirat), munadat (hayya ala), and munajat (la ilaha illa Allah) -- turning happens only during munadat
- **Adhan from a high place is sunnah** -- today fulfilled by speakers on minarets
- **Walking around the minaret** is only allowed during Hayya ala al-Salah/Falah and only if the muadhin can't reach all directions from one spot
- **Placing fingers in ears is recommended** but not from the core sunan of adhan -- it relates to voice, not the kalimat
- **Tathweeb** means calling people to salah between adhan and iqamah
- **In Fajr**, tathweeb is recommended due to it being the time of sleep
- **In other salawat**, earlier scholars said makruh, but **today's fatwa is ja'iz** in all prayers due to increased ghaflah
- **Any customary words** can be used for tathweeb -- not restricted to specific phrases
- **Special tathweeb for leaders/scholars** is supported by the practice of Bilal going specifically to the Prophet (peace be upon him)
