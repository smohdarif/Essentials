*A simplified summary of the lecture explaining when we cannot pray and why*

## The Three Forbidden Times

The teacher started by reminding us of three specific times of the day when we are not allowed to pray:

**1. At sunrise (Tulu' al-Shams)** -- when the sun is rising

**2. At midday (Zawal)** -- when the sun is at its highest point, right above our head, just before it starts going down. By the way, "Zaheera" means midday -- that's why "akl al-Zaheera" means lunch in Arabic.

**3. At sunset (Ghurub)** -- this isn't from the moment the sun looks reddish. It's the very last bit, just before the sun starts hiding from view. You can almost feel Maghrib is about to come in.

During these three times, we also can't pray the **funeral prayer (Salat al-Janazah)**. But -- and this is important -- **burying the deceased itself is fine**. It's only the funeral prayer that is disliked at these times.

---

## How Long Do These Forbidden Periods Last?

The teacher explained how scholars used to figure this out before modern science:

**The old method:** They would stick a pole in the ground and draw a circle around it. They would watch the shadow. As the sun rises, the shadow shrinks. When the shadow stops shrinking and starts growing on the other side -- that's the moment Zawal ends.

**The modern method:** Astronomers calculate it using **longitudes** (the imaginary lines running north-south on the globe). The sun takes about **4 minutes** to pass from one longitude to the next. The longitude lines on a globe are drawn every 15 degrees, so 15 x 4 = 60 minutes = 1 hour. That's how time zones work.

**So technically Zawal only lasts about 4-6 minutes.** But scholars (fuqaha) added extra time as a safety precaution (called **ihtiyat** -- being cautious). So they say:
- Wait about **10 minutes** after sunrise before praying
- Avoid about **10 minutes** at midday
- The window at sunset is shorter (3-5 minutes) because Maghrib needs to be prayed quickly anyway

**A note about countries:** Big countries like China or Russia cover many longitudes, but they pick just one official time for the whole country. That's why we use one standard prayer time for our city instead of every neighborhood having its own.

## A Big Disagreement With Imam Shafi'i

This is where it gets interesting. Imam Shafi'i (a great scholar) says: *"You can pray missed obligatory prayers (Qada Fard) during these forbidden times."*

The Hanafis say: **No, you cannot pray during these times -- not even missed obligatory prayers.**

### How does Imam Shafi'i argue against us?

He brings a **hadith from Sahih Bukhari** that says:

> *"If anyone of you can get one sajdah of Fajr before the sun rises, he should complete his prayer."*

(Note: "one sajdah" here means "one rak'ah.")

The opposition says to us: **"This hadith is in Sahih Bukhari! How can you ignore it?"**

This is a strong attack on the Hanafi position. Why? Because Sahih Bukhari is considered one of the most reliable hadith books.

---

## Important Sidebar: What Makes a Hadith "Top Tier"?

The teacher took a moment to explain some hadith terminology:

**Muttafaqun Alayh** means "agreed upon" -- when both Imam Bukhari AND Imam Muslim included the same hadith in their collections. This is considered the highest level of authentic hadith.

But here's a key lesson the teacher emphasized: **Just because a hadith is in Sahih Bukhari doesn't automatically mean it overrides every other hadith.** Other hadith books, like Imam Malik's *Muwatta*, are also extremely reliable. We shouldn't be **mutaassib** (blindly biased) toward one collection. Use evidence (daleel) properly -- be **firm** in your view, not **fanatical**.

---

## How the Hanafis Answer

The Hanafis don't just throw away the Bukhari hadith. They use it differently. Here's their response:

**Answer 1: There's another hadith!** From Sahih Muslim, the Prophet (peace be upon him) said when you see the redness of sunrise, **stop praying**. Why? Because the sun rises between the two horns of Shaytan (symbolically). So we have hadith from Sahih Muslim on our side too.

**Answer 2: Reinterpreting the Bukhari hadith.** When the hadith talks about "catching one rak'ah before sunrise" -- we say this means: *the prayer becomes obligatory on this person*. For example:
- A woman whose period ends just before sunrise -- Fajr becomes obligatory on her
- A child who reaches puberty at that moment -- Fajr becomes obligatory
- A non-Muslim who accepts Islam at that moment -- Fajr becomes obligatory

So the hadith isn't saying "go ahead and pray during sunrise" -- it's saying "the obligation is now on you" (and you'll pray it after the forbidden time passes).

---

## The Real Problem: Two Hadiths Disagreeing (Ta'arud)

Now we have a serious situation:

- **Sahih Muslim:** Don't pray until the sun has fully risen
- **Sahih Bukhari:** If you catch a sajdah before sunrise, complete your prayer

Both are top-tier authentic. Neither can be easily preferred over the other. This situation has a name in Islamic studies:

**Ta'arud** = *Two hadiths of equal strength that seem to contradict each other.*

When this happens, here's the step-by-step procedure scholars follow:

### Step 1: Tatbiq (Reconciliation)
Try to combine both hadiths so they both work together. If you can do this -- done!

### Step 2: Tarjih (Preference)
If you can't combine them, try to prefer one over the other based on some reason.

### Step 3: Naskh (Abrogation)
If you can't even prefer one, see which came later in time. The later one cancels (Naskh) the earlier one. The cancelled one is called **Mansukh**, and the active one is called **Nasikh**.

How do we know which came later? Several clues -- for example, looking at which Companion narrated it and when they accepted Islam.

---

## A Tough Counter-Argument Against the Hanafis

The opposition says something even stronger:

> *"You Hanafis are being inconsistent! For sunrise, you say if a person's prayer time hits sunrise mid-prayer, the prayer becomes invalid (fasid) and they must stop or repeat it later. But for Asr, you say a person can complete it even as the sun is setting. You're using one part of the hadith and ignoring the other part. Who gave you this authority?"*

This is a strong attack. The Hanafi response involves doing **Tatbiq** (reconciliation) between the hadiths -- finding a way that respects both. We don't ignore Sahih Bukhari; we use Sahih Muslim alongside it.

The teacher mentioned that **Allama Anwar Shah Kashmiri** (a great scholar) gave an excellent answer to this whole issue in his commentary on Sahih Bukhari. He promised to read that explanation in the next class.

## Other Related Points

**Imam Shafi'i** also says you can pray any prayer in **Makkah** at any time -- even during these forbidden times. The Hanafi view: even in Makkah, the rules apply.

**Imam Abu Yusuf** (a Hanafi scholar) holds a slightly different view: voluntary prayer is allowed during Zawal **only on Fridays** (Jumu'ah). This is because of a narration suggesting the Prophet (peace be upon him) or the Companions prayed Jumu'ah right around Zawal time.

**Why is the funeral prayer included with regular prayer?** Because it counts as "prayer" (Salah). Similarly, **Sajdah of recitation** (Sajdat al-Tilawah) is also forbidden in these times because it requires wudu, just like Salah does.

**Why is midday especially a forbidden time?** One reason mentioned in some hadith: this is when the angels change shifts. Some angels are leaving and others are arriving at the middle of the day.

## From the Hidaya Text

This section talks about a third category called **"The Deficient/Incomplete Time"** -- times when prayer is not allowed because of something specific about that time itself.

### The Three Forbidden Times for Prayer

Prayer is **not permitted** during these three times of the day:

1. **When the sun is rising** (sunrise)
2. **When the sun is at its highest point** (zenith / midday) -- right before it starts to descend
3. **When the sun is setting** (sunset) -- from when it turns reddish/orange until it fully disappears

### The Evidence (Hadith)

This ruling comes from a saying of **Uqbah ibn Amir** (a companion of Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him), who reported:

> *"There are three times during which the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) forbade us from praying or burying our dead:*
> - *When the sun is rising, until it has risen high*
> - *When the sun is at its peak, until it begins to decline*
> - *When the sun is leaning toward setting, until it has fully set."*

### An Important Clarification

When the hadith says *"burying our dead,"* it actually refers to **the funeral prayer (Salat al-Janazah)** -- not the actual act of burial. This is because:
- Performing the funeral prayer at these times is **disliked**
- But the **actual burial** itself is **not disliked** at these times

### A Scholarly Debate

The author then mentions that this hadith is used as evidence in two scholarly disagreements:

- **Against Imam Shafi'i**: Regarding whether obligatory prayers (like missed Fard prayers) can be made up during these forbidden times. The Hanafi view (which this book follows) says no, even for obligatory prayers.

- **Against Imam Abu Yusuf**: Regarding praying in **Makkah** during these times. Abu Yusuf allowed prayer in Makkah at all times, but the main view here is that even in Makkah, these time restrictions apply to both obligatory and voluntary prayers.

---

### Quick Summary

- **Don't pray during:** sunrise, exact midday, sunset
- **You CAN bury the dead** at these times -- but **don't pray Janazah**
- **Hanafi view:** No prayer at all during these times -- not even missed obligatory ones
- **The big debate:** A Sahih Bukhari hadith seems to allow prayer at sunrise, but a Sahih Muslim hadith forbids it
- **Solution:** Both hadiths are used together (Tatbiq), and the Bukhari hadith is interpreted to mean "the prayer becomes obligatory" -- not "go ahead and pray now"
- **Lesson on attitude:** Be confident in your evidence, but never dismiss the other side's evidence without a real reason
