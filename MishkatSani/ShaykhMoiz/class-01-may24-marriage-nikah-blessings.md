## The Four Qualities of a Good Wife

The teacher began by summarizing four qualities that define a good wife according to the hadith:

1. **She is muti'ah (obedient)** -- she is obedient and cooperative with her husband
2. **She is pleasing to her husband** -- both in terms of outward beauty and inner beauty (beauty of character)
3. **She prays her five daily prayers regularly** -- fulfilling her rights towards Allah
4. **She protects her chastity in the absence of her husband** -- guarding herself when he is not present

These four points form a complete summary of what it means to be a good wife.

### Jannah Made Easy for Women

The teacher explained that Allah has made the path to Jannah relatively easier for a woman. For a man, the responsibilities are extensive: earning a livelihood, taking care of the home, going out for da'wah, inviting others towards deen, jihad, amr bil ma'ruf wa nahi anil munkar -- all of this requires striving, struggling, and sacrificing.

But for a woman, when she gets married, if she fulfills two things:

1. She **pleases Allah** -- prays regularly, fulfills her rights towards Allah
2. She **pleases her husband** -- lives such a life where her husband is happy with her

Then according to a hadith, **Allah opens all eight doors of Jannah** for her and says: enter through whichever door you want.

The teacher's own sheikh, **Shaykh Azajir Ahmed**, used to say: for a woman to enter Jannah, she just needs to do two things -- please Allah on one side, and please her husband at home.

### A Note on Physical Beauty

The teacher pointed out that Islam does not completely sideline or ignore the dimension of **outward beauty**. It is important for a healthy marriage. A person should keep themselves outwardly beautiful -- and if going to a salon or doing makeup is done with the right intention (for the sake of one's husband), it even becomes **rewarding**. So the hadith encompasses both outward beauty and inner beauty (beauty of character).

## Marriage is Half of Iman

The next hadith discussed was the well-known teaching:

> When a servant marries, he has perfected/completed **half of his religion**. Now let him fear Allah in the remaining half.

### Why is Nikah Half of Iman?

A person has two types of rights and responsibilities:

1. **Huquq Allah** (rights towards Allah) -- this is one half of our deen
2. **Huququl Ibad** (rights towards people) -- this is the other half

Within the rights towards people, the relationship of nikah is so **delicate and sensitive** that if a person fulfills this relationship properly, it is expected and assumed that they would also be fulfilling all their other huququl ibad. The Prophet (peace be upon him) said:

> *"The best of you are those who are best towards their wife."*

So the **yardstick** of huququl ibad is basically how you treat your wife. If a person passes that test, their other relationships will also be healthy and beneficial.

### Before Marriage -- Only 50%

Before nikah, no matter how much ibadah a person does, how righteous and pious they are, they are fulfilling at best only **50%** of their deen. The other half becomes available only when they get married. Once married, the rate of reward also increases -- it is mentioned that even their **salah is multiplied by 27 times** after marriage.

## Islam's Religious Equality for Women -- A Comparison

The teacher shared a comparison he makes when teaching this subject at university, particularly when students raise questions about Islam and women.

### Islam's Position: Complete Parity

Islam gives **complete religious equality** to men and women in two areas:

**1. Spirituality (Taqwa):**
- A woman can be as pious as any man
- There are no extra hindrances for a woman
- As much as a man can be a **wali of Allah**, a woman can also be a wali of Allah
- In the akhirah, just as a man has access to **Jannatul Firdaus**, a woman has equal access

**2. Knowledge:**
- Equal access to learning and acquiring 'ilm

### Contrast with Other Traditions

**Catholicism:**
- Only a man can attain the highest level of spirituality (pope, bishop, cardinal)
- A woman cannot attain that high spiritual rank
- For a man to attain high spirituality, he must **forego women** -- he must practice **celibacy**

**Hinduism:**
- Only a man can achieve the highest spiritual ranks (yogi, Brahman)
- A woman cannot attain those ranks
- Women are viewed as an **obstacle** and **hindrance** in a man's spiritual development
- Men must forego marriage to attain high spirituality

**Buddhism:**
- The highest spiritual state (**nirvana**) is reserved only for men
- Women cannot attain nirvana
- They go even further: if a man even **accidentally touches** a woman, she spiritually **defiles** him

The teacher pointed out the irony: if feminists were evenhanded in their criticism, Buddhism should be number one on their list, followed by Catholicism and Hinduism. Yet in the media, it's all about Islam. This reveals that much of the critique is **politically motivated**, not genuinely concerned with women's rights.

### Islam's Unique Position on Marriage

By contrast, in Islam:
- Men and women can be equally spiritual
- A man does **not** need to forego women to be spiritual
- He actually **needs** to marry to attain the completion of his iman
- Marriage is not detrimental or even neutral -- it is **required** for spiritual completion

## The Greatest Barakah: Simplicity in Nikah

The next hadith was about the recipe for the most blessed nikah:

> *"The greatest of nikkah in terms of barakah (blessings) is that nikah where the burden is the least."*

The teacher emphasized:

- **Aʿẓam** (greatest) -- not just azim (big), but the **superlative** -- the greatest barakah
- The nikah with the **least burden**, the **least effort**, the **most simplicity** -- that is the most blessed

### Practical Application

- Don't make the wedding event a burden for the family
- The **mahr** should be such that the man can easily give
- The functions should not be burdensome for either family
- It's contextual and case-by-case: if Allah has given someone wealth and they can easily spend for their guests, that's fine because it's not burdensome for them
- The general teaching is: **keep it simple, keep it easy**

### What Happens When We Make It Difficult?

When nikah becomes difficult and burdensome, we **lose the barakah**. And the effect of that shows in the marital life itself. The beginnings are very important -- they determine how the marriage will continue. If the beginning follows the teachings of the Sharia, there will be barakah. The more one moves away from these teachings, the more the barakah reduces proportionately.

### The Simplicity of Nikah in the Time of the Sahaba

The teacher mentioned that even during the Prophet's (peace be upon him) time, marriages were extremely simple. Some Sahaba would get married and **wouldn't even invite the Prophet** (peace be upon him) to their nikah. The Prophet would later find out and ask: "Did you just get married?" The Sahabi would say yes, and the Prophet would make du'a for them.

There was no takaluf (unnecessary formality), no elaborate arrangements. Whoever was available, the nikah was done. It could be a small family affair. The more ease, the better.

### Addressing Children's Expectations

A student asked about the challenge of children wanting elaborate weddings because of peer pressure and social media. The teacher acknowledged it's difficult but said:

- One has to gently explain the teachings of the Sunnah
- Teach them that these teachings bring the greatest happiness, joy, and barakah
- It comes down to **tarbiyah** -- raising children in such a way that they have the **azmat (reverence) of deen** in their hearts, love for the Prophet (peace be upon him) and his teachings
- The model for us is the Prophet (peace be upon him), not the lifestyles and trends that keep changing
- They need to be willing to sacrifice customs and traditions for the sake of deen

## New Chapter: Looking at a Prospective Spouse Before Proposal

The teacher began **Bab al-Nazar ila al-Makhtoobah** -- the chapter on looking at the woman to whom one is sending a proposal.

### The Ruling: It is Mustahabb (Recommended)

According to the **Hanafi** jurisprudence and most ulama, it is actually **permissible and recommended** for a man to see a woman whom he intends to propose to. The Prophet (peace be upon him) said to a man who intended to marry an Ansari woman:

> *"Go look at her. For there is something in the eyes of some of the Ansar."*

The commentary explains that some Ansari women had a condition in their eyes which might later become a source of disliking or discomfort. So it's actually better to see before proposing.

### Why Looking is Important

1. **Physical attraction matters** -- Sharia takes this into consideration. If there is no physical attraction, it is difficult to have a healthy marriage, no matter how spiritual both people are. Allah has made individuals in such a way that physical attraction is **one of the elements** in the equation -- not the only element, but an important one.

2. **Even without permission** -- the Hanafi position goes to the extent that if a man is serious about proposing, he can look at her even if she doesn't give explicit permission. However, this comes at a **later stage**, not as the first step.

### The Practical Process

1. **First:** A man can ask his female relatives -- mother, sister, or someone trustworthy -- to go and gather necessary information, see the woman, and report back on whether there's compatibility
2. **Later:** If everything looks good, then there should be a **physical meeting** where the man actually sees the woman

### What Can Be Seen?

When looking at the prospective spouse, one looks at:
- The **face**
- The **hands**

She does not need to be in niqab for this meeting. She can uncover her face and he can see her. She can also look at him. But other parts must remain concealed.

### Can Pictures Be Exchanged Beforehand?

A student asked whether sharing photos before the physical meeting is permissible. The teacher said **yes, it would also be okay** beforehand. However, even if a person has already made their decision through photos, the teacher's own teachers used to say: **still go for the physical meeting** -- just to do **amal upon the Sunnah** and to fulfill the teaching of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and receive the ajr of acting on the Sunnah.

### What If They Say No After Meeting?

A student raised the concern that saying no after a physical meeting can be difficult and have negative emotional impact. The teacher acknowledged this but said: **it is better to say no than to be stuck** in an unfortunate marriage where you said yes without being sure. Being upfront, though difficult, is better in the long run.

## A Woman Should Not Describe Another Woman to Her Husband

The final hadith discussed was:

> *"A woman must not come in close contact (touch the body of) another woman without a garment in between. And she must not describe another woman to her husband, because describing her to him is as if he was looking at her."*

Women should be careful about this: **do not describe another woman to your husband**. Doing so could put **waswasah** (whispers/thoughts) in the husband's mind, and the woman who described her would be held sinful and responsible.
