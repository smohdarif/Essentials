## Recommended Surahs in Each Prayer

The teacher began by discussing what the Prophet (peace be upon him) used to recite in each prayer. The books of fiqh mention specific recommendations:

- **Fajr:** From **Surah al-Buruj** (Juz 30) to longer surahs -- the Prophet (peace be upon him) would recite lengthy portions
- **Zuhr:** Up to **Surah al-Zilzal** -- medium-length surahs
- **Maghrib and 'Isha:** The sunnah of the Prophet (peace be upon him) would sometimes vary

The teacher noted that although these recommendations are written in the books and scholars mention them, the Prophet (peace be upon him) would sometimes vary what he recited -- so these are general guidelines showing **jawaz** (permissibility), not strict obligations.

### Abu Bakr al-Siddiq and Surah al-Baqarah in Fajr

A narration mentions that **Abu Bakr** (may Allah be pleased with him) once recited **Surah al-Baqarah** in the Fajr prayer. The teacher explained this could only have been possible if he started the prayer right at the **beginning of the Fajr time** -- and even then, he must have recited at a slightly faster pace, because finishing Surah al-Baqarah at a very slow speed in two rakats of Fajr would not be feasible before the time expires.

This also shows that the Sahaba used to begin prayer right at the starting time of Fajr.

### Umar ibn al-Khattab and Surah Yusuf / Surah al-Hajj

**Abdullah ibn Abd al-Rabbih** narrated that they prayed behind **Umar** (may Allah be pleased with him), and he recited **Surah Yusuf** and **Surah al-Hajj** in the Fajr prayer. The narrator described it as being with **slow qira'ah** -- but the teacher clarified that even so, he couldn't have been reading at an extremely slow pace to finish both surahs. It was slow in the sense of measured and clear, but still at a pace that allowed him to complete them.

When asked about this, the narrator confirmed that Umar began when the **Fajr time had just entered** -- right at the start.

### What the Prophet (peace be upon him) Would Read

A Sahabi mentioned:

> "I cannot count how many times I heard the Prophet (peace be upon him) recite **Surah al-Tin** and **Surah al-'Alaq** (or similar short surahs) in the two rakats of Maghrib and the two rakats of (the sunnah of) Fajr."

Another narration states that the Prophet (peace be upon him) used to pray with the **Tiwal al-Mufassal** surahs -- meaning the longer surahs from the Mufassal section (from **Surah al-Hujurat** to the end of the Quran). No small or big surah from the Mufassal section was left unread by the Prophet (peace be upon him) in the obligatory prayers.

## The Major Discussion: Qira'ah Behind the Imam

This was the central topic of the class. Should the person praying behind the imam (the muqtadi) recite Quran or not?

### The Hadith

The Prophet (peace be upon him) was praying, and the recitation was becoming difficult for him -- as if the Quran was being "snatched" from him. After finishing the prayer, he turned to the Sahaba and asked:

> *"Does any of you recite with me from the Quran?"*

A person said: "Yes."

The Prophet (peace be upon him) said:

> *"What is happening to me -- it is as if someone is snatching the Quran from me."*

In another wording, the Prophet (peace be upon him) said:

> **لَا صَلَاةَ لِمَنْ لَمْ يَقْرَأْ...**

*"There is no prayer for the one who does not recite..."*

After hearing this from the Prophet (peace be upon him), the narrator says: **the people stopped** reciting behind the imam in those prayers where the imam recites aloud.

### The Hanafi Position: No Qira'ah Behind the Imam

The **Ahnaf** (Hanafi scholars) say: the muqtadi does **not** recite anything behind the imam -- not Surah al-Fatiha, not any other surah -- in **any prayer**, whether it is a **jahri** (loud) prayer or a **sirri** (quiet) prayer.

Their proofs:

**1. The hadith above** -- the Prophet (peace be upon him) clearly indicated that reciting behind him caused difficulty, and the Sahaba stopped doing it.

**2. The Quran itself:**

> **وَإِذَا قُرِئَ الْقُرْآنُ فَاسْتَمِعُوا لَهُ وَأَنْصِتُوا**

*"And when the Quran is recited, listen to it and be silent."* (Surah al-A'raf 7:204)

The teacher broke this ayah down into two commands:

- **فَاسْتَمِعُوا لَهُ** -- "Listen to it" -- this applies in the **jahri** (loud) prayers like Fajr, Maghrib, and 'Isha, where you can actually hear the imam
- **وَأَنْصِتُوا** -- "And be silent" -- this applies even when you **cannot hear** the imam, like in the **sirri** (quiet) prayers like Zuhr and 'Asr. Even though the Quran is not being recited aloud, it is still being recited -- so be quiet.

**3. The hadith:**

> **إِنَّمَا جُعِلَ الْإِمَامُ لِيُؤْتَمَّ بِهِ**

*"The imam is only appointed to be followed."*

So when the imam recites, the muqtadi follows by listening and remaining silent.

**4. The hadith:**

> **إِنَّ الْمُصَلِّيَ يُنَاجِي رَبَّهُ**

*"The one praying is whispering with his Lord."*

So one should be mindful of what he is "whispering" to Allah and not raise voices over one another with the Quran -- meaning the muqtadi should not be reciting simultaneously with the imam.

### The Shafi'i Position

**Imam Shafi'i** holds that reciting **Surah al-Fatiha** is obligatory for the muqtadi as well -- even behind the imam. They have their own proofs for this.

### The Maliki Position

**Imam Malik's** view is that in the **sirri** (quiet) prayers, the muqtadi should recite. But in the **jahri** (loud) prayers, he does not recite.

### Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal's Position

**Imam Ahmad** held that if the muqtadi can hear the imam's voice, he does not recite. If he cannot hear, he should recite.

### Ijma' al-Sahaba

The teacher noted that the claim of **ijma' al-Sahaba** (consensus of the Companions) is brought -- that all the Sahaba held the view that the muqtadi should not recite behind the imam. A student asked about this, and the teacher confirmed: yes, this is stated. But then why do other imams differ?

The answer: the other imams (Shafi'i, Malik, Ahmad) had **proofs that were stronger in their view** -- proofs from the Quran and Sunnah that, in the hierarchy of Islamic legal evidence, rank above ijma' al-Sahaba. So while ijma' al-Sahaba is the third level of proof (after Quran and Sunnah), they found direct textual evidence to support their positions.

## The Hadith of the Man Who Didn't Know Quran

A man from the tribe of **Juhainah** came to the Prophet (peace be upon him) and said:

> *"I am not capable of memorizing anything from the Quran. Teach me something that will be sufficient for me."*

The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: Say:

> **سُبْحَانَ اللهِ وَالْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ وَلَا إِلٰهَ إِلَّا اللهُ وَاللهُ أَكْبَرُ وَلَا حَوْلَ وَلَا قُوَّةَ إِلَّا بِاللهِ**

*"Glory be to Allah, praise be to Allah, there is no god but Allah, Allah is the Greatest, and there is no power nor strength except with Allah."*

The man then gestured with his hands, gathering them together, and said: *"This is all for Allah. What is for me?"*

The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: Say:

> **اللَّهُمَّ ارْحَمْنِي وَعَافِنِي وَاهْدِنِي وَارْزُقْنِي**

*"O Allah, have mercy on me, protect me, guide me, and provide for me."*

The man then gathered both his hands (joining them together). The narrator said he "filled up both his hands with good" -- meaning he received abundant blessings from these simple words.

### Imam Shafi'i's View on Dhikr in Prayer

The teacher mentioned that according to **Imam Shafi'i**, when a verse of the Quran comes during recitation that calls for seeking refuge or making du'a, the imam **can** respond with the appropriate dhikr (like saying **أَعُوذُ بِاللهِ** or making a supplication). However, in the **Hanafi** view, the imam cannot do this -- only the **munfarid** (person praying alone) can respond to such verses during his own prayer.

The teacher gave the example of **Sheikh Hassan Ali** (an imam in America) who, when reaching a verse about seeking refuge from punishment, would say something in Arabic -- this follows the Shafi'i practice.

### Responding to Specific Verses

The teacher mentioned specific verses where a response is recommended (for the munfarid in the Hanafi view):

- When reaching **أَلَيْسَ اللهُ بِأَحْكَمِ الْحَاكِمِينَ** (*"Is Allah not the best of judges?"* -- Surah al-Tin 95:8), one should say: **بَلَى** ("Yes indeed") and give testimony

- When reaching **أَلَيْسَ ذٰلِكَ بِقَادِرٍ عَلَى أَنْ يُحْيِيَ الْمَوْتَى** (*"Is He not able to give life to the dead?"* -- Surah al-Qiyamah 75:40), one should say: **بَلَى** ("Yes indeed")

- When reaching **فَبِأَيِّ حَدِيثٍ بَعْدَهُ يُؤْمِنُونَ** (*"After this, in what message will they believe?"* -- Surah al-Mursalat 77:50), one should say: **آمَنَّا بِاللهِ** ("We believe in Allah")

### The Prophet (peace be upon him) Reciting Surah al-Rahman to the Jinn

The teacher shared a beautiful narration. The Prophet (peace be upon him) came to his companions one day and recited **Surah al-Rahman** from beginning to end. The Sahaba were all quiet.

The Prophet (peace be upon him) then said:

> *"I recited it to the jinn on the night of the jinn (Laylat al-Jinn), and they were better than all of you in replying."*

Every time the Prophet (peace be upon him) reached the verse:

> **فَبِأَيِّ آلَاءِ رَبِّكُمَا تُكَذِّبَانِ**

*"So which of the favors of your Lord will you two deny?"*

The jinn would respond:

> **لَا بِشَيْءٍ مِنْ نِعَمِكَ رَبَّنَا نُكَذِّبُ ، فَلَكَ الْحَمْدُ**

*"We do not deny any of Your favors, O our Lord! For You is all praise."*

## A Man from Juhainah: Reading Surah al-Ikhlas in Both Rakats

A man from the tribe of Juhainah mentioned that he heard the Prophet (peace be upon him) reciting **Surah al-Ikhlas** (Qul Huwa Allahu Ahad) in **both rakats** of a prayer. The teacher noted this could have been done for a specific reason, or it could show **jawaz** -- that it's permissible to repeat the same surah in both rakats. Sometimes the Prophet (peace be upon him) would do things to demonstrate permissibility.

The teacher also connected this to a point made in earlier classes: sometimes when the Prophet (peace be upon him) would hear babies crying behind him, he would **shorten the prayer** out of mercy for the mothers praying in the congregation.

## Why Are There Two Sajdahs in Each Rakat?

The teacher discussed why we prostrate **twice** in every rakat, while we only bow (ruku) **once**. Several reasons were given:

### 1. Closeness to Allah

There is a hadith that says:

> *"A person is closest to Allah when he is in sajdah."*

Because sajdah is the position of **utmost closeness**, it is done twice -- to increase that nearness.

### 2. Man's Creation from Clay

Human beings were created from **sand/clay** (turab). And to the earth we shall return. The sajdah -- placing the forehead on the ground -- is a return to our origin. The two sajdahs reflect this connection.

### 3. The Story of the Angels and Iblis

When Allah commanded all the angels (and Iblis) to prostrate to Adam (peace be upon him):
- All the **angels prostrated**
- **Iblis (Shaytan) refused** to prostrate

When the angels raised their heads and saw that Iblis did not prostrate -- that he did not receive the **tawfiq** (divine enabling) while they did -- they prostrated a **second time out of gratitude** (shukr) to Allah for granting them the tawfiq to obey.

So the second sajdah is a **sajdah of shukr** -- thanking Allah for enabling us to prostrate in the first place.

## Ta'dil al-Arkan: Equal Timing of Prayer Postures

This was an important practical lesson. The teacher discussed several ahadith emphasizing that the **ruku, sajdah, jalsa (sitting between two sajdahs), and qawmah (standing after ruku)** should all be of **equal duration**.

### The Hadith

A Sahabi narrated that the Prophet's (peace be upon him) prayer had the following postures all at the **same timing**:

1. **Sajdah** (prostration)
2. **Jalsa** (sitting between two sajdahs)
3. **Qawmah** (standing after ruku, before going into sajdah)
4. **Rising from sajdah**

All four were equal in length.

### The Hadith of Prolonged Qawmah and Jalsa

When the Prophet (peace be upon him) used to rise from ruku, he would stand so long (in qawmah) that the Sahaba would think:

> *"Maybe he has forgotten -- maybe he forgot he needs to go into sajdah now."*

And when he would sit between the two sajdahs (jalsa), he would sit so long that the Sahaba would again think:

> *"Maybe he has forgotten -- maybe he forgot he needs to go into the next sajdah."*

This shows that the Prophet (peace be upon him) made the **qawmah and jalsa noticeably long** -- not the quick up-and-down that most people do today.

### The Practical Lesson

The teacher emphasized: today, most people make their ruku and sajdah reasonably long, but the **jalsa and qawmah are rushed** -- sometimes less than a second. This goes against the sunnah.

The teacher urged: make your **jalsa** (sitting between sajdahs) and **qawmah** (standing after ruku) the **same length** as your ruku and sajdah. All four postures should be balanced and equal. Don't just go up and down quickly -- take your time.

## The Three Speeds of Quran Recitation

A student asked about the types of recitation speeds. The teacher explained three categories:

1. **Tartil** -- very slow, measured recitation. This is what reciters like **Sheikh al-Minshawi** and **Sheikh Abdul Basit** are known for -- a very deliberate, slow pace
2. **Tadwir** -- the **medium** speed. This is what most imams use in Fajr, Maghrib, and 'Isha nowadays -- a balanced, moderate pace
3. **Hadar** -- faster recitation. This is what you commonly see in **Taraweeh** prayers, or in classes where students are reciting their lessons to the teacher at a quicker pace

## Q&A: Why Do Different Imams Have Different Rulings Despite Ijma' al-Sahaba?

A student asked: if the hadith narrator says all the Sahaba agreed (ijma') on not reciting behind the imam, why do Imam Shafi'i and Imam Malik differ?

The teacher explained the **hierarchy of Islamic legal proofs**:

1. **Quran** (highest)
2. **Sunnah** (hadith of the Prophet, peace be upon him)
3. **Ijma' al-Sahaba** (consensus of the Companions)

The other imams had proofs from the **first and second levels** (Quran and Sunnah) that, in their assessment, were stronger than the ijma' of the Sahaba. So they followed the higher-ranking evidence.

The teacher also clarified about **Imam Malik's Muwatta**: Imam Malik includes ahadith in his book that both **support** and **counter** his own madhab. So finding a hadith in the Muwatta doesn't automatically mean it supports the Maliki position -- Imam Malik was honest in presenting all the evidence.

## Quick Summary

- **Recommended surahs:** Fajr with longer surahs (Buruj onwards), Zuhr up to Zilzal; the Prophet (peace be upon him) varied his recitation
- **Abu Bakr recited Surah al-Baqarah in Fajr** -- possible by starting right at the beginning of the time
- **Umar recited Surah Yusuf and Surah al-Hajj in Fajr** -- also from the very start of the time
- **Hanafi position: no qira'ah behind the imam** -- not Fatiha, not anything, in any prayer (jahri or sirri)
- Proofs include: the Quran (listen and be silent), the Prophet's discomfort when Sahaba recited behind him, and the hadith "the imam is appointed to be followed"
- **Shafi'i:** Fatiha is obligatory behind the imam; **Maliki:** recite in sirri, not in jahri; **Hanbali:** recite if you can't hear the imam
- **For someone who can't memorize Quran:** say SubhanAllah, Alhamdulillah, La ilaha illallah, Allahu Akbar, La hawla wa la quwwata illa billah
- **Responding to Quran verses in salah** (like بَلَى or آمَنَّا بِاللهِ) -- Hanafis allow this only for the munfarid, not the imam; Shafi'is allow it for the imam too
- **Jinn responded better than the Sahaba** to Surah al-Rahman's repeated verse
- **Two sajdahs:** closeness to Allah, return to clay, and the angels' gratitude sajdah after seeing Iblis refuse
- **Ta'dil al-arkan:** ruku, sajdah, jalsa, and qawmah should all be **equal in length** -- don't rush the jalsa and qawmah
- **Three recitation speeds:** Tartil (slow), Tadwir (medium), Hadar (fast)
